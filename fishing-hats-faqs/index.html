Fishing Hats questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best fishing hat for UV protection is one with a wide brim (at least 3 inches), a dark underside to reduce glare, and a fabric rated UPF 50+ ─ ideally in a breathable, quick-drying material like polyester or nylon with a mesh crown for ventilation.

What makes a hat good for fishing?

A fishing hat needs to do at least three things: block harmful UV rays, keep you cool, and stay put in wind or when wet. The most important feature is a wide, stiff brim that shades your face, ears, and the back of your neck. Many anglers also want a dark fabric under the brim to cut glare off the water. Look for hats made from lightweight, quick-drying synthetics such as polyester or nylon. Cotton soaks up sweat and takes forever to dry, making it miserable on a long day on the water. A mesh or vented crown helps air circulate so you don’t overheat. The best hats also include an adjustable chin strap or a security lanyard. If the wind kicks up or you lean over the side to land a fish, you don’t want your hat floating away. Some models even have a floating foam insert built into the brim.

How does UV protection work in a hat?

Sun protection in a hat comes from both the fabric itself and the hat’s design. Fabric blocks UV rays based on its weave density, color, and material. A tightly woven dark fabric naturally blocks more rays than a loose, light-colored one. But many fishing hats are now labeled with a UPF (Ultraviolet Protection Factor) rating ─ UPF 50+ blocks at least 98% of UVA and UVB rays. The brim shape matters too. A wide, downward-angled brim shields your eyes, nose, and ears. A hat without a dark underside can still let reflected UV bounce off the water into your face. That’s why higher-end fishing hats use a black or dark green fabric on the brim’s underside to absorb stray rays. Even the best hat can’t protect your entire body, so always pair it with sunscreen on exposed skin and polarized sunglasses for your eyes.

What materials are best for a fishing hat?

Synthetic fabrics dominate the fishing-hat world for good reason. Polyester and nylon are lightweight, dry fast, and resist mildew. Many hats also have a DWR (durable water repellent) finish to shed light rain or splashes. Cotton is comfy but a poor choice for fishing because it soaks up water and stays wet. Wool blends can handle cooler weather but won’t dry quickly. Avoid anything with a heavy lining unless you’re fishing in near-freezing conditions. For the crown, look for mesh panels or laser-cut vent holes. A solid fabric hat turns into a sauna in direct sun. A foam-reinforced brim holds its shape even when wet, which is a big advantage over floppy cotton brims that droop in your eyes.

Should I get a wide brim or a cap?

For serious UV protection, a wide-brimmed hat wins every time. Baseball caps leave your ears, the back of your neck, and the sides of your face exposed. Even a cap with a flap in the back is less effective than a full 360-degree brim. That said, a wide brim can be a nuisance in heavy wind, and some anglers find it gets in the way when casting a fly rod. If you primarily fish from a boat where wind is constant, a bucket-style hat with a moderate brim (about 2.5 to 3 inches) might be a better balance. A boonie hat (the kind with a floppy brim all around) is a classic choice ─ it packs down easily and offers solid coverage. Just make sure the brim is stiff enough to hold its shape or has a wire insert so you can adjust the angle.

How should a fishing hat fit?

A fishing hat should fit snugly but not tight. If it leaves a red mark on your forehead after an hour, it’s too small. If it tilts when you turn your head quickly, it’s too big. Look for hats with an adjustable drawcord or a hook-and-loop closure at the back so you can dial in the fit. Most good fishing hats come in a range of sizes (small through XX-large) rather than one-size-fits-all. Avoid a one-size hat with a tight elastic band ─ those tend to slip and cause headaches. Also consider the hat’s internal sweatband. A moisture-wicking band (often terry cloth or a synthetic chamois) helps keep sweat out of your eyes and prevents the hat from getting stinky too quickly.

How to care for a fishing hat?

Rinse your hat with fresh water after every saltwater trip. Salt crystals can damage the fabric and hardware over time. Let it air dry out of direct sun ─ heat from a dryer can shrink synthetic fabrics and warp foam brims. Spot-clean stains with a mild soap and a soft brush. Don’t toss it in a washing machine unless the manufacturer’s tag says it’s safe. Aggressive agitation can break down the UPF coating and distort the brim. Store your hat somewhere it won’t get crushed. A hat clip on your tackle bag or a dedicated hat case keeps the brim from getting bent out of shape. If the brim does get wonky, a quick steam from a kettle (held a few inches away) can help you reshape it, then let it cool in the corrected position.

Frequently asked questions

Do fishing hats really block UV?

Yes, but only if they’re made from a fabric with a UPF rating of 50 or higher and have enough coverage. A standard baseball cap does very little. A wide-brim, dark-underside hat with UPF 50+ fabric will block the vast majority of harmful rays.

Can I wear a fishing hat in the rain?

Most synthetic fishing hats are water-resistant, not waterproof. They’ll shed a light shower just fine, but in a heavy rain the fabric will eventually soak through. If you fish in steady rain, look for a hat with a labeled waterproof membrane or a sealed seam construction.

Are neck flaps necessary?

They’re optional but very useful if you’re out at high noon in open water. A detachable neck cape or a built-in flap adds coverage without forcing you into a full sun hood. Many anglers prefer a flap that zips or buttons on so they can remove it on overcast days.

What UPF rating do I need?

Look for UPF 50+. That blocks 98% of UV rays. Anything lower (UPF 30, for example) still lets in too much radiation for extended exposure. UPF 50 is the highest common rating and the gold standard for serious sun protection.

Can I wash a sun hat?

Hand-wash with cool water and a mild detergent. Avoid bleach, fabric softener, and machine washing unless the tag explicitly says it’s okay. Aggressive washing can ruin the brim shape and degrade the UV-blocking treatment. Always air dry away from high heat.

Do fishing hats float?

Some do. A few popular models are designed with a foam inner layer in the brim to keep the hat on top of the water if it blows off. If you fish from a kayak or a small boat, a floating hat is a smart choice. Check the product description for “floatable” or “snag-free chin strap” features.

In shortA good fishing hat is one of the simplest and most effective pieces of sun gear you can own. Focus on a wide, stiff brim with a dark underside, UPF 50+ fabric, and a secure chin strap. Pair it with polarized sunglasses and sunscreen, and you’ll be comfortable and protected from sunrise to last cast.
